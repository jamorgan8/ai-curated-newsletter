Justice Department Establishes "Anti-Weaponization Fund," Faces Congressional Scrutiny

The Justice Department on Monday announced the creation of a nearly $1.8 billion "Anti-Weaponization Fund" aimed at compensating allies of President Donald Trump who claim they were politically targeted. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche stated the fund provides a "lawful process for victims of lawfare and weaponization to be heard and seek redress." The announcement came as part of a settlement for a $10 billion lawsuit filed by President Trump against the IRS concerning the leaking of his tax returns. Democrats in the House of Representatives have swiftly condemned the move, with nearly 100 members signing a legal brief to block the settlement, calling it an "unconstitutional taxpayer-funded settlement" and a potential "slush fund" for allies. Acting Attorney General Blanche is scheduled to appear before a Senate appropriations subcommittee today for his first congressional testimony since assuming his role, where he is expected to face questions regarding the fund.

Supreme Court Remands Voting Rights Act Cases to Lower Courts

The U.S. Supreme Court on Monday vacated and remanded two voting discrimination cases from Mississippi and North Dakota, sending them back to lower courts for reconsideration. The cases involve challenges to redistricting plans accused of diluting the voting power of Black and Native American citizens, respectively. The Court's decision instructs lower courts to re-examine the cases in light of the earlier "Callais decision," which significantly weakened Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act by limiting the right of individuals to sue. Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson issued a dissent, arguing that the majority's action was unnecessary and further punished voters.

Senate Approves Resolution to Freeze Lawmakers' Pay During Future Government Shutdowns

The U.S. Senate on Thursday approved a resolution that would prevent senators from receiving their paychecks during any government shutdowns that occur after this year's midterm elections. The measure, introduced by Republican Senator John Kennedy of Louisiana, passed by voice vote. While it will not impact members of the House of Representatives, as each chamber sets its own rules, the resolution aims to incentivize timely funding of the federal government. Similar to federal employees, senators would receive their withheld pay once a shutdown concludes.
